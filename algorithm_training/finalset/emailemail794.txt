On Mon, Oct 29, 2012 at 8:08 PM, Shinji Ryu <sryu@college.harvard.edu>wrote:

> Apart from our personal experiences, Korean and Jewish immigrants do have
> a lot of empirical similarities.
>
> For one, the occupations of the first-generation and second-generation
> immigrants of both groups are strikingly similar. Both groups came mostly
> as blue-collar, working-class people (though it is now changing in recent
> years for Koreans; this change is very new: 10~15 years, give or take). The
> most common occupation for adult Korean first-generation immigrants (that
> came relatively early) is the dry-cleaning business. Exactly a generation
> earlier, the Jewish people commonly operated dry cleaners in the East Coast
> (Chinese immigrants on the West Coast). Similarly, many Korean first-gen
> immigrants operated grocery stores (particularly in East Coast
> metropolises), as did the Jews one generation earlier. In many cases,
> Korean immigrants inherited those businesses as the Jewish first-gen
> immigrants retired.
>
> The second-generation of both groups are similar in their occupational
> choices as well. Koreans, like other East Asian ethnicities, almost never
> inherit the occupations of their parents, opting for professional,
> service-sector jobs. Jews, too, followed the same pattern.
>
> Of course, these similarities do not mean that contemporary Koreans and
> Jews can relate to each other. While Koreans and Jews did follow a similar
> path toward assimilation, they largely did not experience it together: Jews
> went through it 30, 40, 50 years before Koreans did. The Korean first-gen
> and contemporary Jews probably couldn't relate; the former would most
> likely be a tailor or dry-cleaner, while the latter would probably be a
> doctor or a lawyer. But those Jewish people's parents could probably relate
> to the Korean first-gen. And most of these similarities are now historical:
> recent immigrants from Korea tend to be financially better-off and more
> skilled, as would be expected of recent Jewish immigrants.
>
